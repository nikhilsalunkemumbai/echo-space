# White Paper 3: Crisis Dynamics and Attribution: The Mechanics of Emergent Instability in Volatility Echo

## Abstract
This paper examines the mechanisms governing crisis generation and attribution within the Volatility Echo Engine. We detail the calculation of community Volatility (V), its probabilistic relationship to crisis events, and the critical role of the `contrib_trace` in assigning pseudo-causality during crisis replay. These dynamics are central to the project's goal of simulating "emergent crises" and "delayed consequences," compelling players to grapple with the inherent difficulty of attributing blame and understanding complex systems in real-world scenarios.

## 1. Introduction
The experience of "emergent crises" is a foundational element of the Volatility Echo Rehearsal Space. Unlike deterministic failures, these crises are designed to arise stochastically from the cumulative effect of community dynamics and player actions, often with delayed and non-obvious causality. This white paper dissects the technical implementation of crisis generation and the attribution system that attempts to shed light on its origins, aligning with the project's constitutional mandate to cultivate an understanding of complexity.

## 2. Volatility (V) Update Mechanism

The core driver of crisis dynamics is the community's `Volatility (V)`, a hidden state variable detailed in White Paper 1. Its evolution is governed by a specific update equation within the `VolatilityEchoEngine.step()` method, designed to simulate memory, shock response, and background noise:

### 2.1. The Volatility Equation

The `V` update rule is formulated as:

`V_{new} = (λ * V_{old}) + (α * |shock|) + η`

Where:
*   `V_{new}`: The new volatility value for the current turn.
*   `V_{old}`: The volatility value from the previous turn.
*   `λ` (Lambda): A decay factor (`self.params["LAMBDA"]`, e.g., 0.8). This represents the memory of past volatility, ensuring that tension does not dissipate instantly.
*   `α` (Alpha): A shock scale factor (`self.params["SHOCK_SCALE"]`, e.g., 0.3). This determines how strongly an incoming `shock` influences `V`.
*   `|shock|`: The absolute value of the `shock` generated by the current turn's post. This `shock` is calculated as `post.emotional_load * (1.2 if action == "QuickReact" else 0.6)`. Notably, the player's "QuickReact" action amplifies the shock's impact, modeling the potential for overreaction to escalate tension.
*   `η` (Eta): A random noise component (`np.random.normal(0, self.params["SIGMA_NOISE"])`, e.g., N(0, 0.01)). This introduces a degree of unpredictability, reflecting real-world stochasticity and preventing players from fully predicting `V`'s trajectory.

After this calculation, `V` is floored at `0.0` to prevent negative volatility.

## 3. Crisis Probability and Triggering

Crises are not directly triggered by exceeding a fixed `V` threshold. Instead, the `V` level influences the *probability* of a crisis occurring in any given turn. This probabilistic model (`VolatilityEchoEngine._crisis_probability()`) reflects the non-deterministic nature of real-world social breakdowns.

### 3.1. Sigmoid Probability Function

The probability of a crisis (`p_crisis`) is calculated using a sigmoid function:

`p_crisis = 1 / (1 + exp(-(V - CRITICAL_V) / CRISIS_WIDTH))`

Where:
*   `V`: The current community Volatility.
*   `CRITICAL_V`: A central point for crisis probability (`self.params["CRISIS_CENTER"]`, e.g., 1.5). As `V` approaches and exceeds this value, `p_crisis` increases sharply.
*   `CRISIS_WIDTH`: Controls the steepness of the sigmoid curve (`self.params["CRISIS_WIDTH"]`, e.g., 0.25), determining how quickly `p_crisis` rises around `CRITICAL_V`.

In each `step()`, a random number is drawn, and if it is less than `p_crisis`, a crisis is triggered.

## 4. Crisis Attribution and `contrib_trace`

When a crisis is triggered, the system attempts to provide a narrative "crisis replay" to offer insights into its potential origins. However, in line with constitutional principles, this attribution is not a definitive blame assignment but rather a plausible connection to a past event.

### 4.1. `contrib_trace`

The `contrib_trace` (a `deque` with a `maxlen` defined by `MAX_TRACE` in `constants.py`) is a history of significant events that could contribute to future crises. Each entry in the trace typically stores:
*   The `turn` number when the event occurred.
*   The `shock` value generated by the post.
*   The `poster_id` of the agent who made the post.

### 4.2. Simplified Attribution Logic (Current Implementation)

In the current `VolatilityEchoEngine._check_crisis()` implementation, if a crisis occurs and the `contrib_trace` is not empty, the crisis replay simply references the *oldest* entry in the `contrib_trace` (`self.contrib_trace[0]`). This simplification serves the purpose of demonstrating "delayed consequences" by linking a current crisis to an earlier action, without needing a complex causal inference model. The `ARCHITECTURE.md` describes a more sophisticated `select_replay_post` function weighting past contributions by shock, emotion, and recency, which represents a future refinement.

The output of the crisis replay is a narrative hint (e.g., "[red]A past tension resurfaces.[/red]") without explicitly naming the agent or post details, preserving the ambiguity and requiring player interpretation.

## 5. Conclusion
The crisis dynamics within the Volatility Echo Engine are carefully constructed to provide a challenging and insightful rehearsal experience. Through the continuous update of Volatility based on emotional shocks and player actions, the probabilistic triggering of crises, and the simplified yet effective attribution mechanism, the system effectively simulates the non-linear and often opaque nature of social instability. This design forces players to consider long-term impacts, manage systemic tension, and interpret complex causality, thereby fulfilling the project's aim of fostering a deeper understanding of emergent phenomena in digital communities.
